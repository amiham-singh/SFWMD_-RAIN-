In 2011, Florida ignited an epidemic.

Here is how.



Between 2001 and 2010, Florida’s refusal to regulate pill mills created a pipeline of illicit oxycodone and other pills to users and traffickers across the United States.

In the first half of 2010, drug distributors shipped 40.8 million oxycodone pills to Florida doctors.


Every other state combined got only 4.5 million.

0

Florida pill mills sold the oxycodone to users and traffickers from other states.
Other states cracked down on their pill mill problems.
Florida didn't.
As long as Florida pill mills stayed open, oxycodone deaths kept rising, here and in other states.

In 2010, The Drug Enforcement Administration reported Florida pill mills were spreading oxycodone in the Southeast, Great Lakes, Northeast and Middle Atlantic regions - virtually every state east of the Mississippi.


A national oxycodone ring even bought South Florida pills to sell in Denver, Salt Lake City, Washington, Oregon, Texas and Alaska.

In 2011, after years of ignoring the crisis, Florida started arresting doctors, cracking down on bogus clinics and tracking oxycodone and other opioid prescriptions.

Oxycodone became harder to get. People began switching to a much cheaper, easier-to-get and equally powerful substitute:

Why would people switch from a prescription drug to a black-market street drug?
The molecular structure of oxycodone and heroin are nearly identical. So is their high.

East of the Mississippi illicit Florida oxycontin had flowed freely. Now, heroin death rates rose sharply as people switched drugs.
West of the Mississippi, Florida oxycodone was not as common. Heroin deaths dropped.



But in seven states where Florida oxycodone had been trafficked, heroin deaths rose by a collective 27 percent



Dig deeper and a more patterns emerge

In 2012, the first full year after the crackdown the rate of heroin deaths rose 104 percent in the Southeast, the first leg of the OxyExpress. Oxycodone-related deaths dropped 11 percent.
